Ancient Times

Ancient Beginnings Long before Palawan became a global travel destination, its shores were home to early settlers such as the Tagbanua and Palaw’an peoples. These indigenous communities relied on the sea for sustenance, using traditional fishing methods and trading with neighboring islands. Archaeological finds, like the Tabon Caves artifacts, suggest that humans have lived along Palawan’s coasts for tens of thousands of years.

Early Inhabitants

The first people of Palawan were indigenous groups such as the Tagbanua, Palaw’an, and Batak. They lived near the coasts and depended on the sea for fishing, transportation, and daily life. For them, the beaches were not tourist spots but sources of food and shelter.

Tourism and Modern Transformation

From the late 20th century onward, Palawan’s beaches — like El Nido’s white sands, Coron’s hidden lagoons, and San Vicente’s Long Beach — began attracting both local and international travelers. Tourism brought economic growth, but also challenges in preserving the fragile marine ecosystems. Today, many communities balance livelihood with conservation, ensuring that the beaches remain pristine for future generations.

Colonial Encounters

During the Spanish colonial period (16th–19th centuries), Palawan’s beaches served as landing points for galleons, missionaries, and traders. Coastal villages became hubs for barter, while some remote coves provided refuge for locals resisting colonial control. The beaches also played a role in maritime defense, with watchtowers built to guard against pirate raids.
